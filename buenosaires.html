Images from Buenos Aires' Bike Share

History


Under the administration of Mayor Mauricio Macri, the Sustainable Mobility Plan ("Plan de Movilidad Sustentable" in Spanish) was formulated to reorgainze traffic in an efficient manner so that Buenos Aires residents can access different modes of well-organized transport systems and have a low impact on the environment. It emphasized the use of non-motorized transport, such as bicycles, to decrease congestion, reduce accidents, and discourage car use. One of the strategies was to develop Ecobici, a bike share system with dedicated cycleways. The "Sistema de Transporte Publico de Bicileta" law ("Public Bicycle Transportation System") that was was passed in 2007, along with the Sustainable Mobility Plan that came afterwards, paved the way for the start of the bike sharing program called "Mejor en Bici" ("Better by Bike") by December 2010.

By the end of 2012, two years after the inauguation of the cycling program, the share of cycling increased from 0.4% to 2%. More than 135 km of protected bike lanes have been added to Buenos Aires since inauguration.

For more information on Buenos Aires' bike share, please go to the Buenos Aires tourism website on cycling in the city (in English).

Interesting Facts



  • By 2015, biking represents more than 3.5% of all trips in the city. That is equivalent to more than 180,000 people biking every day as their main mode of transportation or to complement other alternatives such as subway, train, or buses.

  • When Ecobici first opened to the public in 2010, it offered just three stations and 72 bicycles, all operated out of manual stations. Since then, the system has expanded to 32 stations with 850 bikes. The system's expansion will eventually feature 200 automated stations, 24-hour service, and 3,000 bicycles when completed.

  • According to the report Copenhagenize Index 2015 of Bicycle-Friendly Cities, Buenos Aires ranks 14th among the 122 urban centers defined as most bike-friendly in the world.

Bike Share Rates



The Buenos Aires city government's Ecobici program provides free bikes for up to an hour at a time. If you want to continue to use the system, you must wait 15 minutes before picking up a new bike.

Bring a photocopy of your passport ID page and immigration stamp and follow the instructions on the following website: http://www.buenosaires.gob.ar/ecobici/sistema-ecobici/turistas.






Popular Routes



  • Start in Plaza Italia in Palermo and take the bike lane on Jorge Luis Borges which becomes Serrano all the way up through the Palermo Soho and Villa Crespo. Here you can stop for nice cup of coffee. Afterwards, go on the bike lane on Padilla and continue to Bravard until you reach Parque Centanario in the neighborhood of Caballito. This park is surrounded by secondhand bookstalls and has a weekend market where you can watch locals enjoy their day.

  • Another route from Plaza Italia, you can go to Bosques de Palermo (Parque Tres de Febrero) to see the rose gardens by biking down Sarmiento. Then, you can take the bike lane all the way down Avenida Lebertador to Plaza Retiro. This route will take you pass the Jardín Japonés, Plaza de Chile, the Museo Nacional de Bellas Artes and Plaza Francia. Across the plaza, go on the bike lane and then left onto San Martin and right on Avenida Antartida Argentina to Puerto Madero. You can cycle around the docks, ride pass Puente de la Mujer, and then get to Reserva Ecológica Costanera Sur to stop for a bondiola (pork shoulder) sandwich from one of the parrilla stands along Avenida Int. Hernán M. Giralt. If you still have time, you can bike around the 8 km reserve where you will see butterflies, rare birds, and wild flowers, with views out across the Río de la Plata.
